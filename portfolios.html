Portfolios

Your guide to creating a strong portfolio

A portfolio is a curated collection of 3–5 projects, often presented as a website. It showcases your skills, process, and problem-solving abilities. More than a gallery of work, your portfolio tells the story of your growth, creativity, and unique approach to solving problems. Employers and clients use it to understand your qualifications and assess fit for roles or projects.

Core components of a portfolio

  • Home Page
  • About Me Page
  • Case Studies that include:
    • Summary or Overview
    • Timeline
    • Your Role
    • Tools, Methods, and/or Skills
    • Context/Background
    • Problem Statement & Research
    • Personas & Design Process
    • Final Design/Outcome
    • Lessons Learned or Reflection
  • Resume (linked PDF)
  • LinkedIn (linked profile)
  • Optional: Art, graphic design, or other creative work

General portfolio guidelines

  • Use templates — no need to code from scratch, but customize to show your style.
  • Don’t publish unfinished pages. Outline next steps or keep them unpublished until ready.
  • Keep navigation clear and simple across devices.
  • Avoid clip art and unrelated stock photos — use visuals from your actual work.
  • Use animation or motion sparingly and always with accessibility in mind.
  • Proofread carefully and get a second pair of eyes to review.
  • Use professional, field-specific language (e.g., “design sprint” instead of “class project”).

Tips for student or entry-level portfolios

  • Start a personal project to demonstrate your skills:
    • Redesign a physical product (e.g., a coffee mug for smaller hands).
    • Improve an app you use regularly (functionality, pain points, design).
    • Show your process and steps taken, not just the final result.
  • Include class projects and explain what you’ve learned, even if not finished.
  • Highlight personal projects if you don’t yet have work or internship experience.

Dealing with NDAs (Non-Disclosure Agreements)

Even if you’re bound by an NDA, you can still share what you learned. Create or modify artifacts to illustrate your process without revealing confidential information. Focus on your role, skills, and reflections.
